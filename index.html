Job Market Paper

A Model of Bank Failures: Funding Frictions and the Dynamics Before Collapse

This paper develops and quantifies a model of bank failures to study how funding frictions shape the dynamics of balance-sheet deterioration and default. Empirically, following previous work in the literature, I replicate that U.S. banks approaching failure systematically shift their funding structures toward time deposits and other costly liabilities, while profitability, leverage, and credit quality deteriorate. Motivated by these patterns, I build a model in which heterogeneous banks face limited commitment, capital requirements, and costly access to illiquid long-term funding. Banks issue both short- and long-term liabilities to smooth cash flows and manage liquidity risk, but doing so increases future repayment burdens and default exposure. The model generates endogenous default thresholds and replicates the principal empirical regularities observed prior to failure. Quantitatively, the framework highlights how the composition of liabilities governs banks’ resilience to shocks: institutions that rely more heavily on time deposits can sustain higher leverage and smoother cash flows, but become more vulnerable once profitability weakens. Long-term funding thus plays a dual role—providing stability when liquidity conditions tighten while amplifying fragility as fundamentals deteriorate. The results show that funding structure is a central dimension of bank fragility and a key determinant of financial stability.
